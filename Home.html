Why should I hire someone with a development background for a UX position?

UX encompasses a lot of great things: empathy, usability, objectivity. But one of UX's greatest assets is its efficiency in process. I like to define UX as “data-driven design” because, through research and testing, feedback is gathered early on and throughout the product development cycle. That means would-be usability problems, would-be customer service calls, and would-be feature revisions get caught early on, saving everyone time and sanity.

So what can a developer turned UX designer do to increase this efficiency even more?

Coded Prototypes

Coding can maximize prototyping efficiency and responsive designs can be easily achieved through the use of frameworks such as Bootstrap. A coded prototype can have interactions that are more accurate to the final product than non-coded prototypes, allowing for more controlled experiments during user testing. They are also is easy to change, easy to make fully interactive, easy to iterate on, and easy to hand off to developers.

DRY Designs

If you’ve ever programmed, you’re probably familiar with DRY, or “don’t repeat yourself”. A good developer seeks to create an elegant codebase with lots of reusable code. This same technique can be applied to design. A developer turned UX designer sees each widget and behavior as a reusable action. No need to solve the same problem repeatedly; a new problem or design decision should be compared to the existing solutions before something new is added. This isn’t just good for product maintenance, it’s good UX. A product that has a consistent look and behavior will feel more trustworthy to users.

Intermediary Between Designers and Developers

There’s often a feeling of separation between these teams, but there really shouldn’t be. They are two sides of the same coin. Having a UXer with a development background can make designs more technically feasible, inspire an interest in “how the other side lives”, and allow for more productive conversions between designers and developers. This can keep expectations realistic and frustrations down.

Conclusion

Development might not be the first background one considers for UX, but a development skillset can bring a host of benefits to a product team.
